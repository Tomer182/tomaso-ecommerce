## The One Thing Most People Get Wrong

Everyone focuses on **what to do at bedtime.** That's not where good sleep happens.

**Good sleep is built during the day.** Your bedtime routine is just the final 5% of a 24-hour process.

## The 24-Hour Sleep System

### Morning (6 AM - 12 PM): Set Your Clock

Your body runs on a circadian rhythmâ€”a 24-hour biological clock. Light is the reset button.

**What to do:**
1. **Get bright light within 30 minutes of waking** (ideally sunlight, 10-15 minutes outside)
2. **No sunglasses during morning light exposure**
3. **If it's dark/cloudy, use a 10,000 lux light box** for 20-30 minutes

**Why it works:**  
Bright light in the morning triggers cortisol release (goodâ€”you want to be alert) and sets a timer for melatonin release 14-16 hours later. This is your body's natural clock.

**What I do:**

### Afternoon (12 PM - 5 PM): Don't Screw It Up

This is where people sabotage their sleep without realizing it.

**The caffeine cutoff:**  
Caffeine has a half-life of 5-6 hours. If you drink coffee at 4 PM, half of it is still in your system at 10 PM.

**My rule:** No caffeine after 2 PM. Sounds early, but it works.

**Movement matters:**  
Exercise improves sleep qualityâ€”but timing matters. Morning or early afternoon is ideal. Evening intense exercise can make it harder to fall asleep.

**What I do:**  
- Workout by 4 PM latest
- Last coffee at 1:30 PM (I have one or two more, but decaf)
- 10-minute walk after lunch (helps with afternoon energy crash without caffeine)

### Evening (5 PM - 9 PM): Wind Down Properly

This is where most "sleep hygiene" advice focuses. It matters, but only if you did the earlier stuff right.

**Dim the lights (seriously):**  
Bright overhead lights in the evening suppress melatonin. Your body thinks it's still daytime.

**What to do:**
- Switch to lamps/dim lighting after sunset
- Reduce screen brightness (or use night mode)
- Avoid bright bathrooms (I installed a dimmer switchâ€”$15, game-changer)

**The temperature drop:**  
Your body needs to drop 2-3 degrees (Fahrenheit) to fall asleep. Help it along.

**What I do:**
- Dinner by 7 PM (digestion raises body temp)
- Hot shower 60-90 minutes before bed (counterintuitive, but the cool-down after helps sleep)
- Keep bedroom at 65-68Â°F (feels cold at first, but you'll sleep way better)

**The phone problem:**  
Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin. But honestly, the bigger issue is **content activation**â€”scrolling social media or reading news fires up your brain.

**My rule:**  
- Phone on charger in another room by 9 PM
- If I must use it, blue light filter + no social media / news
- Reading (physical book or e-reader with warm light) is fine

### Night (9 PM - 11 PM): The Routine That Actually Works

Forget complex 15-step routines. Here's what matters:

**The wind-down hour:**  
60-90 minutes before bed, start signaling to your body: "We're done for the day."

- 9:45 PM: Read in bed (warm light, fiction onlyâ€”nothing work-related)

**If you can't fall asleep in 20 minutes:**

## The Common Mistakes (And How to Fix Them)

**Mistake 1: Inconsistent sleep schedule**  
Going to bed at 10 PM Monday, 2 AM Friday, 11 PM Sunday = confusing your circadian rhythm.

**Fix:** Same sleep and wake time every day (yes, weekends too). Painful at first, transformative after 2 weeks.

**Mistake 2: Napping wrong**  
Long naps (>30 min) or late naps (after 3 PM) destroy nighttime sleep.

**Fix:** If you must nap, 15-20 minutes max, before 2 PM.

**Mistake 3: Alcohol to "help sleep"**  
Alcohol might make you drowsy, but it destroys sleep quality. You get less REM sleep and wake up feeling terrible.

**Fix:** If you drink, finish 3+ hours before bed. Better yet, skip it.

**Mistake 4: Working/watching TV in bed**  
Your brain starts associating bed with alertness instead of sleep.

**Fix:** Bed is for sleep and sex only. Work, TV, scrollingâ€”do those elsewhere.

**Mistake 5: Stressing about sleep**  
Lying awake thinking "I need to sleep or tomorrow will suck" activates your stress response, which prevents sleep.

**Fix:** Accept that occasional bad nights happen. One bad night won't ruin you. Stressing about it will.

## The Tracking That Helps (And What Doesn't)

I used a sleep tracker (Oura Ring) for a year. Here's what I learned:

**Useful data:**
- Sleep duration trends
- Sleep consistency trends
- Whether changes actually improved sleep

**Not useful:**
- Obsessing over daily scores
- Trying to "optimize" every metric
- Letting a number tell you how you feel

**My approach:** Check weekly averages. Ignore daily fluctuations.

**You don't need a tracker to improve sleep.** Just track subjectively:
- How long to fall asleep? (aim for <20 min)
- Wake up during night? (aim for 0-1 times)
- Feel rested in morning? (aim for yes)

If all three improve over 2-3 weeks, your changes are working.

## When to See a Doctor

Some sleep issues need professional help:

- **Snoring loudly + daytime exhaustion** (possible sleep apnea)
- **Trouble sleeping 3+ nights per week for 3+ months** (chronic insomnia)
- **Unusual movements or behaviors during sleep** (parasomnia)
- **Falling asleep randomly during the day** (possible narcolepsy)

Don't tough it out. These are medical conditions with treatments.
